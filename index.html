Supreme Mathematics

A 5% nation lesson

According to "The Five Percenter "Way of Life" by Felicia Miyakawa, five percenter men and women have committed the numbers (in reference to Supreme Mathematics) and their meanings to memory. The numbers being 0-9, five percenters will use these numbers to "creatively show and prove facts and ideas" (pg 200). These numbers can arise anywhere, but for the purpose of this website, we will display the best usage of each number within the song titles itself (not limited to just 5 percenters). To the right is each number and its respective meaning.



0: Cipher



3005, Childish Gambino

decade: 2010s

The song “3005” by Childish Gambino was released in 2013 and uses two zeroes in the title. Roughly a year after the song was released, a secret remix was discovered by fans by piecing together lines of code that were in a screenplay based around the music on his album (Fleischer). The secret remix is known as the Beach Picnic Version and features the same chorus as the original version, just in a higher pitch, and includes a slower tempo and a more relaxed vibe. In a religious context, the idea of a cipher may be interpreted as seeking deeper meaning, as religious truths can be revealed to those who do the work to decode surface-level symbols, texts, and traditions. For 3005, fans that were willing to put in the work to decode ciphered messages in the screenplay were rewarded with the secret remix. It is clear that the significance of the number zero extends beyond the title of the song and that the screenplay associated with the song was a cipher for fans.


1: Knowledge



One Mic, Nas

decade: 2000s

One Mic is the third single off of Nas’ fifth studio album, Stillmatic, released in December of 2001. Although not a Five Percenter himself, Nas is thought to have embodied some of NGE’s teachings in his music. One Mic is Nas’ furthest venture into Supreme Mathematics. Throughout the song, Nas leverages the ‘One’ in the song’s title and main refrain to drop knowledge. The song features dense and oftentimes intense verses, discussing police violence in inner cities, which is especially apparent in the song’s music video. Eventually, the song is brought back down to lower heat by the soft delivery of the chorus: “All I need is one mic”. Outside of the number one, the track is littered with allusions to Supreme Mathematics with repeated two and nines. Additionally, the following two tracks on Stillmatic are “2nd Childhood” and “Destroy & Rebuild,” inconspicuous references to NGE wisdom and the number 8, respectively. Yet, Verse 2 dives deeper into the Fiver Percenter way, calculating supreme values in his bars: “Jesus died at age thirty-three, there’s thirty-three shots/From twin Glocks, there’s sixteen apiece that’s thirty-two/Which means one of my guns was holdin’ seventeen/Twenty-seven hit your crew, six went into you.” Ultimately, One Mic is an instance of Nas leveraging Five Percenter theology to discuss his own faith, appealing to ‘One God,’ ‘virgin blood,’ and ‘Jesus’ to grant his wish for a simple life and to be absolved of sin. Here, Nas in communion with NGE uses ‘One’ to acknowledge the truth of a situation and reroute the situation towards the fundamental simplicity of God, which he views as the solution.


2: Wisdom



The Heart Part 2, Kendrick Lamar

decade: 2010s

The Heart series by Kendrick Lamar is a journey through his life and career serving as benchmarks for an emotional ride of each of his music. Specifically part 2 is Kdot rapping about his city of Compton, having moments where he prioritizes genuine emotion over simply stringing impressive lyrical content. He aims to convey raw emotion and connect deeply with his audience through his lyrics. Wisdom is the ability to make sensible decisions and give good advice because of the experience you have.

Because of Kendricks upbringing he is able to display to the listener what he has been through and how it currently influences his decisions, even making a connection with God in the process. “I ain’t perfect / I ain’t seen too many churches or know them testament verses”, Kendrick now defines himself as Christian, and this is early in his career but he acknowledges his imperfections and lack of religious upbringing showing self-awareness and humility. “My uncle doing life inside prison; he wasn’t wrapped too tight / He told me ‘Rap about life, not rap n****s” he provides wisdom, literal advice from his incarcerated uncle recognizing his own importance of rapping about real-life experiences. The heart series allows listeners to better connect with Kdot on an emotional level, “ Im tryna purchase my momma dem’ Benzes / Occupying my time with riches, justifying my time ambitions / Just to coincide, just to go inside, plus we idolize why we living”. Here we see his motivations for success, displaying his material desires while also questioning the deeper meaning and purpose behind them.


3: Understanding



3 Kings, Rick Ross, Dr.Dre, Jay-Z

decade: 2010s

3 Kings is the third track off of Rick Ross’ 2012 album “God Forgives, I Don’t,” featuring two other hip-hop and rap legends in Dr. Dre and Jay-Z. 3 Kings was made long after the peak of Five Percenter popularity in the hip-hop scene and this distance is evident in the lack of meaning placed behind numbers cited on the record. However, the track is an important reminder of the influence of NGE theology, and religious doctrine at large, in hip-hop music. Nas’ One Mic offers a great foil to 3 Kings. Both tracks use numbers in the same frequency and fashion, often and at beginning or end of a bar. In verse 3, Jay-Z rhymes “They workin’ on another deal, they talkin’ two-hundred-fifty!/I’m holding out for three/Two-seventy-five and I just might agree.” Clearly, Ross’ song lacks the spiritual element that Nas’ masters, using Supreme Mathematics as a concept but not for any specific purpose. Between the divine mathematics of NGE and the Christian story of the birth of Jesus, 3 Kings holds a lot of spiritual potential, but ignores it beyond an aesthetic title. The most sympathetic reading of 3 Kings is with respect to Five Percenters, using the number three to emphasize the understanding of three hip-hop sages between Ross, Jay, and Dre.


4: Culture and Freedom



Jesus Walks, Kanye

decade: 2000s

Additionally, the number four represents culture and freedom. The song “Jesus Walks” is the fourth track off Kanye’s 2004, debut album The College Dropout. As the fourth track on the album, “Jesus Walks” portrays religious themes and bridges the gap between mainstream rap culture and religion. At the time, the themes that dominated mainstream hip-hop were understood as explicitly non-religious. Drugs, alcohol, sex, money, gangs, and violence were the themes associated with mainstream hip-hop, enforcing its distance from religious themes. “Jesus Walks” was nominated at the 2005 Grammy awards for the song of the year, adding to the notion of the song achieving mainstream success while promoting religious themes. Moreover, the second version of the music video to “Jesus Walks” heavily references the freedom that one receives by way of their belief in Christ. The video depicts a fire around Kanye but it is unable to hurt him because he had God protecting him, essentially saying that those with God are free from the tortures of hell. In the context of religion, culture and freedom are relevant for how individuals form and express unique religious beliefs. The freedom to develop and express individual religious beliefs is important and each person’s beliefs will be developed from the culture by which they are surrounded. In the context of “Jesus Walks”, Kanye challenged expectations and norms in the hip-hop industry, showcasing the freedom artists have to explore diverse subject matter and express their beliefs. Also, by promoting religious themes, Kanye bridged a cultural divide and sparked conversations about spirituality and religion within hip-hop. In the context of the music video, Kanye makes it clear that those who have Jesus are free. As the fourth track on The College Dropout, “Jesus Walks” affirms the number four’s significance in both freedom and culture.

6: Equality



The 6th Sense, Common

decade: 2000s

6th Sense is the first single from Common’s 2000 album Like Water for Chocolate. Common is a known and celebrated Five Percenter, a fact which comes through musically and thematically on the track. 6th sense, similar to Nas’ One Mic, leverages divine mathematics for a socially conscious and spiritual goal. Throughout the track, Common is set on communicating the social ills of the world, calling himself “Morpheus in this hip-hop Matrix, exposing fake shit.” This bar is a clear allusion to the 6 in the song title, striving for equality of knowledge, wisdom, and understanding as each verse works to analyze his world critically as well as introspectively and offer that conclusion to the audience. One instance of this is the opening of verse 2, where Common raps “In front of two-inch glass and Arabs I order fries.” Here, Common points out the issue of Arab business proprietors in his area not wanting to engage with the black community, including the ‘two’ as a hint of this. Additionally, the track is comfortable being overtly religious and existing in the intersection of NGE and the Nation of Islam as Common raps “through me, Muhammad will forever speak,” aligning his words with the truth of a prophet in harmony with the number six’s representation of equality.

8: Build-Destroy



Straight Outta Compton, NWA

decade: 1980s

N.W.A’s album, Straight Outta Compton was released on August 8, 1988 or 8/8/88. The album protests police violence in Los Angeles, acting as a source of both building and destruction. The album received a lot of backlash for its vulgar language and violent themes. In a Los Angeles Times article titled “The Moment N.W.A Changed the Music World,” Gerrick Kennedy writes, “‘Straight Outta Compton’ was a flash point that spoke for a disenfranchised community and disrupted the order of those who were confronted with the voices and images of a community they’d much rather ignore” (Kennedy). The confrontational nature of the album was inherently destructive in its attempt to disrupt the oppressive systems that lead to police brutality. As a source of destruction, the album challenges an oppressive system in an attempt to disrupt and destroy the systems that lead to police violence. Furthermore, Kennedy explains how the title of the track “Fuck Tha Police” was explicitly used to protest police brutality during the late 80s and early 90s and ultimately remained “a rallying cry in L.A.” during the Black Lives Matter movement. As a source of building, the album sparks conversations around social change and seeks to build communities. In a religious context, the ideas of building and destruction are relevant in personal transformation, where people seek to destroy the old version of themselves and build a new, better version of themselves. This is similar in the case of this album, where N.W.A seeks to destroy current systems in place and build new, less oppressive systems. Releasing the album on 8/8/88 confirms the number eight’s importance related to the concepts of building and destruction.


9: Born



99 Problems, Jay-Z

decade: 2000s

99 Problems is the third single off of Jay-Z’s 2003 album, The Black Album. Like Nas, Jay-Z has never come out as a member of the Nations of Gods and Earths but has carried the influence of their teachings in his music. In addition to the double 9 in its title, the track is positioned as the 9th listing on the album, emphasizing the Five Percenter concept of birth. Moreover, Jay-Z creates the implication of the number 1 and consequently, knowledge by using the number 99. The combination of birth (9) and knowledge (1) create a full circle moment for Jay-Z, who was at the time announcing his retirement from hip-hop. His fascination with both of these themes also pairs well with the socially-conscious attitude of the song, exposing police mistreatment and more.
